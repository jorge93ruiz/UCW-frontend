When wāhine connect and unite, magic happens

Uniting Canterbury Women connects women of all ethnicities, cultures and religions at a series of exciting live and online events, and through our various online platforms.

Our purpose is to break down barriers, and provide a welcome space where women can connect, communicate, collaborate, enjoy events, workshops and activities together, learn from each other, and work together to make a difference in our communities.

We also love to champion the great works and initiatives of other Canterbury wāhine, and to tell the stories of inspiring wāhine in our community in our regular newsletter, on our website, and through our social media channels.

Making the perfect Christmas pavlova!
Jo shares her top tips and favourite recipe for making the perfect festive pavlova.

Despite being the classic Kiwi Christmas dessert, until last year, I had never actually made a pavlova myself! My brother was the pavlova maker in our family, and after he sadly passed away last year, I decided to make one for our family Christmas, to honour his memory. I was a little nervous about giving it a go for the first time, but by following these top tips gleaned from a good recipe, and chatting to my expert pavlova making friend – it turned out brilliantly. I hope yours does too! Pavlova Simple tips for achieving the perfect pavlova: 1. Use older eggs (at room temperature) as the whites break down easier 2. Wipe your bowl beforehand with lemon juice to remove any greasy residue 3. Use caster sugar and not regular sugar as the fine crystals trap and hold more air 4. Beat your mixture for at least 10 minutes 5. Have your oven on regular bake (not fan bake) 6. Leave the pavlova in the oven for ages after it's cooked Recipe: 6 egg whites (at room temperature) 2 cups caster sugar 1 tsp vanilla essence 1 tsp white vinegar 2 tsp cornflour 300m cream whipped, plus fruit, chocolate etc for decorating Pre-heat over to 110 degrees celsius (not fan bake) Line a baking tray with baking paper. In a large metal, ceramic or glass bowl (not plastic), beat the egg whites until soft peaks form. Continue beating while gradually adding the caster sugar - a quarter of a cup at the time. (I beat each quarter of a cup in for a minute and a half before adding the next one). The mixture should become thick and glossy. Once all the sugar has been beaten in (minimum 10-12 minutes in total), beat in the vanilla, vinegar and cornflour. Spoon the mixture onto the prepared tray, and shape similar to the example in my photo. Bake for approximately 1.5 hours, until dry and crisp and the pavlova lifts easily off the baking paper. Turn the oven off and leave the pavlova inside for at least an hour before removing from the oven (I recommend leaving for much longer - 4-5 hours, or even longer). When completely cool, place on a serving dish, add whipped cream and toppings of your choice. NB: Don't be surprised if the crust is a bit broken looking when it comes out of the oven. I was worried mine had failed to start with but when we cut into it, the centre was soft, light and fluffy. And the toppings hid any broken bits on top! You can make your pavlova the day before you intend to serve it, but it's a good idea to eat any leftovers within 24 hours of serving.

Welcome packs for Afghan evacuees
Our major initiative of 2021 – gifts to help our newest Kiwis feel at home.

Uniting Canterbury Women was devasted to hear about the plight of the evacuees who fled Afghanistan to New Zealand at the end of August to escape the brutal new Taliban regime. Many men, women and children went through an incredibly traumatic time to get here and arrived with little more than the clothes on their backs. We were determined to do something to help, so started a Givealittle campaign to raise money to create individual Welcome Packs for the evacuees at the Mangere Refugee Resettlement Centre. This was a cause especially close to our hearts, as two of our current crew members, Zahra Hussaini, and Suhayla Asghari are former refugees from Afghanistan. They both spent time at the Centre on their arrival to New Zealand, and never forgot the kindnesses they received. We easily surpassed our goal to raise $11,000 – reaching over $14,000 in the end thanks to great support from Kiwi donors. Our campaign also attracted the support of generous social enterprises and businesses who either donated goods for the packs, or offered wholesale prices or less. Even former Prime Minister, Helen Clark got behind the Givealittle campaign, mentioning it almost daily on her social media platforms, and there was a story on 1News at Midday talking about the initiative. In early October, with the help of some awesome women, we packaged up around 250 very special Welcome Packs, filled with items that would introduce the evacuees to their new country, and also remind them of home. The packs included fragrant spices, saffron, and tea which was specially blended by Zahra. The women were gifted beautiful silk krama (scarves) handwoven in Cambodia, Linden Leaves products and TradeAid bags, among many other items, while prayer mats, woollen hats and thermos flasks were among the items in the men's packs. We were so excited to hear that the packs arrived in Auckland, and can’t wait for the feedback from the Centre. Head to our Facebook page to check out all the stories and photos of the people and businesses/social enterprises who supported us throughout this wonderful journey.

How we started founders

Uniting Canterbury Women was founded after the tragic terror attack in Christchurch on 15 March 2019.

Co-founder, Jo Bailey reached out to her only Muslim friend at the time, Zahra Hussaini, to talk about how to continue on the beautiful love and support for the Muslim community being shown by people in Canterbury and New Zealand, and Uniting Canterbury Women was born.

Zahra introduced Jo to four of her friends, Rebecca Parnham, Dr Hafsa Ahmed, Amber Johnson, and Naeema Masud. The six women bonded over coffee and cake, and together, organised the inaugural Uniting Canterbury Women event, which was attended by nearly 600 women.

Today, Jo Bailey and Rebecca Parnham continue to lead Uniting Canterbury Women and organise a range of events to connect wāhine across all cultures. They are supported by the rest of the UCW team, and new crew member, Suhayla Asghari, when they are available to assist.

Uniting Canterbury Women has a popular Facebook page with over 1250 followers. Zahra Hussaini runs the group's thriving Instagram page.

Many incredible connections and friendships have been made and continue to be made across the Uniting Canterbury Women community.

Plains FM
Plains FM does an incredible job amplifying the voices of migrant and other communities in Christchurch. We talk to Nicki Reece and Laura Gartner about their incredible mahi.

Amplifying voices and empowering communities In a relatively non-descript brick building on bustling Madras Street, some incredible community work has been going on for over 35 years, and continues to reach every corner of Ōtautahi Christchurch and beyond. The building is home to Plains FM, a multi-award winning local radio station, which over its long history has been a platform for diverse perspectives, amplifying the voices of people from all walks and life and backgrounds. Do you have a great idea for a radio show? At Plains FM there is no barrier to you becoming a broadcaster and joining the current hosts of more than 90 radio shows in 20 languages being produced at the station. This is perhaps the most remarkable aspect of Plains FM, that anyone truly can become a broadcaster and bring their idea to life, with the encouragement, support and training of the station’s team. At the helm of this extraordinary endeavour is Station Manager, Nicki Reece, who joined Plains FM way back in 1989, just a year after it was founded. She had just finished a course in Radio Broadcasting, heard about a production role at Plains FM, had a chat to the station manager at the time, and the rest is history. Nicki has managed Plains FM since 2007, and her dedication, passion and hard work was rewarded with a New Zealand Radio Award for Services to Broadcasting in 2021. “I was overwhelmed. It was a beautiful thing, and felt like it wasn’t just celebrating my journey, but all those people who had come along with me over my time here,” says Nicki. Plains FM is one of 12 stations in New Zealand which is part of the Community Access Media Alliance (CAMA). It produces a range of broadcasts to provide for the interests of women, youth, children, persons with disabilities and minorities in the community, including ethnic minorities. “Migrant communities hold a special place in our programming and are definitely a priority group. Their shows celebrate culture, language, traditions and effectively reach and connect with people within their communities,” says Nicki. Content Co-ordinator, Laura Gartner has been employed by Plains FM for 9 years, but was a broadcaster back in 1993 with the Earthwise environmental show. During her time in the role, she has been responsible for introducing countless new broadcasters to the airwaves, including many women from migrant communities. “I come from a social justice background and it is really important to me that marginalised voices are part of the media landscape. You don’t find this in mainstream media, but here at Plains FM, people can present their own stories and ideas in their own language, in their own style and there are no barriers. Equity of access is so important.” A few days after the terror attack on 15 March 2019, Nicki and Laura met with local journalist, Lana Hart, to talk about how Plains FM could do its bit to support the local community through the trauma. The result was a show called After March 15th, which went on to win Best Access Radio Programme at the 2021 New Zealand Radio Awards. This led to a funded project – the powerful Widows of Shuhada, which followed the journey through grief of four Muslim women widowed by the attack. This show was a finalist in the Best Documentary or Factual Talk Feature category at the awards. Also in 2021, Laura was part of the team which produced the show, It’s Getting Hot in Here, about climate change, which won Best Access Radio Programme. Plains FM broadcasts 24/7 and is a much-loved companion for many engaged listeners. Its shows can also be listened to on demand on the station’s website and podcast platforms. One of its longest running shows is a weekly, hour-long Tongan language show called, Faka'amanaki, which Siale Faitotonu has been running for 30 years. “Siale is amazing. He is a super busy man but makes the show every week as a service to his community. It provides important messages in the Tongan language and creates important cultural connections,” says Laura. Other Pasifika content makers have been on air for over 10 and even 20 years. Nicki says these shows were running long before the concept of keeping languages alive really came into vogue, and various Pacific Language Weeks were introduced. “These broadcasters have been committed since way back then to making those connections and maintaining their language and culture through the programmes.” Many broadcasters from ethnic communities record their shows in English, which helps to create strong cross-cultural connections, providing opportunities for English speakers to listen, learn and gain another perspective on the world, says Nicki. A snapshot of Plains FM’s current and new shows highlight the station’s incredible diversity. An upcoming show, Cult Chat, is being made by three women who have survived cult-like environments. As well as telling their own incredible stories, they are interviewing others with similar experiences. In July, Muslim life coach, Fariya Naseem is launching Pep Talk, which provides advice for women in challenging situations. The day Uniting Canterbury Women visited Plains FM’s studios, Mercy Munatsi was working with Laura Gartner on her show called African Renaissance, which plays a variety of stunning African music. Another new show to hit the airwaves is Rock for Recovery, created by Carl, a former mental health specialist, who uses examples of hard rock music to support people through mental health and addiction issues. Many different groups from the disability sector have enjoyed the opportunity to make their own shows at Plains FM, which also supports schools and youth broadcasters. The School for Young Writers has produced a series of radio plays. There are endless creative opportunities to be explored. Nicki says the most popular show in terms of audience numbers is the weekly podcast created by Alcoholics Anonymous, featuring stories of recovering alcoholics. What can you expect if you have an idea for a new radio show at Plains FM? Most new broadcasters start out knowing nothing at all about radio, but if they have an idea or passion, Plains FM can help them turn it into a show, says Laura. “We bring them into the station, show them around the facilities, talk them through the process, and walk them through every step of the way at their own pace, including training on all the elements that go into making a programme. Some people want to work with us on the technical aspects, and many go on to learn to record and edit the shows themselves once they feel comfortable with the technology.” Broadcasters pay a small, very reasonable fee for the training process, and for each 25-minute episode of their shows. As well as her pivotal role bringing new broadcasters into Plains FM’s ever-growing network, Laura has been active out in the community, recording various live events and public speakers, with the recordings later played on the radio. She has recorded everything from engaging lectures at University of Canterbury, to talks by interesting guest speakers at Quaker meetings, and Canterbury Workers Education Association meetings. The Plains FM team even recorded Uniting Canterbury Women’s inaugural event in July 2019. Plains FM is also positively impacting on the education sector. It has recently launched Content Making for Radio and Podcasts, a comprehensive programme, which fits into the New Zealand Curriculum and NCEA frameworks. The programme guides students, particularly those studying English, Media Studies and Digital Technology, right through the audio content production process. “Communication is key these days, but not everyone knows how to make a really good audio show. This resource, which is available for schools to purchase, helps students to make great content, and earn the credits they need to fulfill curriculum requirements. The programme is a great resource for teachers, as it reduces the amount of time they need to spend planning, preparing and organising these projects.” Sadly, Laura's tenure at Plains FM is coming to an end later in June as she heads back to Canada for several months for family reasons. “It has been an absolute privilege and amazing experience to work here. I’ve met so many people who have shared their stories and life stories with me and the station. One of the greatest things is seeing people gain their confidence, after coming into the station for the first time feeling scared and nervous. It’s such a great feeling to be able to support their vision so they can achieve their goals. That’s been a highlight, along with a great team to work with, and the exposure to so many different cultures, and different ways of thinking.” Laura’s departure will undoubtedly leave a void, but the Plains FM team will share her role until a new person is employed, supported by Nicki who will help out with induction and training as needed. “There is a lot of sadness about losing Laura. I will miss her hugely but she leaves such a positive legacy, with her unwavering dedication allowing countless individuals to find their voice and share their stories. I am confident that because Plains FM is such a special place, good people always end up here, and we will ensure that Laura’s legacy of nurturing connections continues on. We are sure the wonderful relationship we have with Laura will continue once she returns to Christchurch. Hopefully she will come back and make another fabulous programme with us.” As she reflects on her 34 years at Plains FM, Nicki is grateful for the ways she has been stretched and grown in many areas in her role, including personal development, the ability to connect with people from all walks of life, keeping up with technological changes, creative thinking, and being flexible. She is rightly proud of the station’s amazing work of the past and present, and is excited as it looks ahead to the future. “Plains FM will continue to evolve and innovate and ensure that everyone’s voice can be heard and celebrated. The legacy of community empowerment will remain at the core of everything we do.”

Fariya Naseem
Fariya Naseem's story is about chasing big dreams, creating her own destiny, and forging a new life for her family in New Zealand.

Living her dreams Fariya Naseem’s inspirational story is about chasing big dreams through sheer will and determination, creating her own destiny rather than being bound by society’s expectations of her, and forging a new life for her family in a country much different to her own. Over a decade ago, back in India, and unable to follow her desired career path because of circumstances, even after gaining two degrees. Fariya made a dynamic decision, with the support of her husband, that would change the course of her family's life forever. It is a story of separation, love, drive, hard work, and unfailing self-belief. Now Fariya is living her dream as a much-loved secondary school Physics teacher, and a certified life coach in Ōtautahi Christchurch, drawing on all the lessons from her own life and struggles as she helps others to reach for their goals and dreams. Fariya’s story begins in Bhopal, India, where she was born to a Science teacher mother, and a police officer father. A lifelong love of reading was shaped by her mother, who regularly bought Fariya and her younger brother Ladybird books to read. “I loved all the fairy tales, and all other stories from Ladybird series. My favourite was Little Red Riding Hood. At times we would be upstairs on the terrace under the stars and mum would narrate the stories of Sindbad the Sailor.” Celebrating various cultures and festivals was an important part of Fariya’s upbringing. Her father placed great emphasis in recognising other faiths and cultures. Muslim by birth, she was raised very multi-cultural. “We celebrated Diwali to Christmas, alongside Eid and Ramadan. Dad made sure that we visited neighbours from other cultures on their festivals and invited them to ours. I have learnt a lot from my upbringing.” Although living in a ‘secure environment with a beautiful family’, Fariya says two life-changing incidents stand out from her childhood. “I was just two years old when the Bhopal gas tragedy struck in 1984. At the time, it was considered the worst industrial disaster in history. Thousands of people died when dangerous gas escaped from the Union Carbide fertilizer plant and covered the city. Even though I was so young, I remember my parents putting a scarf on my nose and wrapping me in a blanket as we fled to my grandparents’ house in a nearby village. We were there for a month or two before we could go home.” As a 10-year-old, Fariya found herself caught up in riots in Bhopal, when police tried to control communal riots. “I had two younger children from my school with me and we ran for our lives. With rioters chasing us I took shelter along with those children in a house closer to my home and saved our lives. It was a while before they would let us into the house. That feeling of protection came naturally to me.” As she grew older, Fariya’s love of books translated into a love of learning. I still get my power from moments like these.” Desperately missing Sadat and Faraj, Fariya worked hard on gratitude and maintaining a positive mindset to get her through. “I would say to myself, I’m so grateful I have a child and he is safe and doing well. I didn’t attach myself to misery. I tried to change my brain chemistry all the time.” Fariya quietly began to explore her Muslim faith in her new city, offering service to the small student mosque on Creyke Road, doing jobs like cleaning the toilets, and folding sheets, and spending time reflecting. Just two months later, the mosque attacks in Christchurch happened.” Half an hour before the terrorist attack on 15 March 2019, Fariya was about to pick up a package near Al Noor Mosque, but decided to take Elnaz out of her car seat and go back inside the house to give her a bottle instead. “Something told me very strongly to do it.” After the attack she went straight into ‘helper’ mode for her community for several months, before exhaustion took over and she gave herself time to work on her health and personal grief around the terrible events. What I have learned is that there is no end to the journey. There is only the journey. And the one thing that is constant on the journey is change.”

Giarne Harrison
Giarne Harrison has been on an extraordinary journey to trace her whakapapa and immerse herself in Māori culture.

Tracing her whakapapa It wasn’t until she was around 18 or 19 years old, that Giarne Harrison (Nō Ngāti Whātua tōkū iwi; Nō Ingarangi, nō Airani hoki ōku tupuna) discovered her Māori,heritage, and the pull to understand her whakapapa and immerse herself in the culture has been powerful ever since. She is currently studying Level 3 te reo Māori, has become a teacher of raranga (weaving), and has pieced together information about her ancestors and their stories, although there are many unanswered questions. “I am still a bit adrift. I can’t name my maunga, awa, or hapū, but I’m trying to be happier about where I stand right now. The journey has helped me to claim all the parts of myself.” Giarne was living in Sydney with her family when her mother first started to speak about her Māori ancestry. It wasn’t until the family moved back to New Zealand in 2001, settling in Christchurch, and Giarne began studying at Christchurch Teachers’ College, that her search for answers began in earnest. “There was a whānau class at the College, and I asked to go into that. When I was asked about my Māori background, I thought our family must be Tainui, because Mum grew up in Morrinsville. At that point I had only done a cursory search into who we were and ended up focusing on the wrong person. I had no concept of how to trace our whakapapa properly and mum didn’t know either.” After making some discoveries and connecting with whānau in the North Island who were on a similar journey, Giarne was able to start putting together more information about her ancestor’s history. “We know very little about my grandmother from five generations back, Raihia Radford. However, it seems likely she ended up in Porirua. Her daughter Eliza boarded from the ages of five to 16 at St Joseph’s Providence Girls’ School, which was part of the Native School system, widespread throughout New Zealand in the mid 1880s.” In a report presented to the Houses of the General Assembly in 1888, St Joseph’s was described as having a striking difference to some of the other schools, in that it was ‘stronger in English but weaker in most of the other subjects.’ It is clear from the report that a key purpose of these boarding schools was to assimilate Māori children into the European way of life. ‘In order that they may receive real training in the European mode of life and have the European customs and modes of thought engrafted into their nature, Māori children must for a time, live as Europeans.’ Once she left school, Eliza went to live with the Cooks, a well-known, Māori/English family from Foxton, most likely as a foster child or employee. The mother, Meretini Cook (born Te Akau), was a step granddaughter of Te Rauparaha, and he was said to have helped engineer her marriage to Thomas Uppadine Cook. They had 13 children and ran multiple businesses. “We know that later Eliza married a baker, Thomas Brown in the Cook’s back yard. Apparently, she was asked to reclaim her lands, but didn’t understand what was being talked about. It appears there was a bankruptcy at some stage that caused issues between Eliza and the Cooks. We also know that in Eliza’s later life, in the early 1900s, she used to travel around and look after her grandchildren.” As a wāhine of English, Irish, Spanish and Māori descent, Giarne understands the complexities of identity. She says it has been important for her to make peace with her Māori ancestors’ assimilation into European culture and the lack of information they have passed down. “I try to feel kindness rather than frustration towards them for assimilating into the dominant culture. I know things must have been tough for them, and they thought it would help them to have a successful life. A part of me still thinks ‘why didn’t you leave more information about where we came from?’ Back in her Teachers’ College days, Giarne says she often felt like an outsider in the whānau class but learnt so much from her classmates. “The immersion of sitting alongside people who had grown up with whānau inside their whakapapa was my first experience of what it would have been like if we had grown up near our whānau. Most of the stuff I learned was through osmosis. I tried at least to be an ally, rather than part of the group. I learned a lot about myself and Te Ao Māori and started figuring out my place in it.” Giarne began learning te reo Māori at Teachers College and has continued her studies at Te Wānanga O Aotearoa, and now with Whitiora (a Ngāi Tahu organisation) where she is currently up to Level 3. During her university studies, Giarne was vice president, and then president of the Students Association at the Christchurch College of Education where she was a strong voice for Māori learners, and second chance learners. She later worked for the NZEI, New Zealand’s largest education union, a Treaty based organisation. Around 10 years ago, Giarne started doing some raranga (weaving) courses, and took to it straight away. “I had never done it before, but it felt right. There is something about the whole sensory experience, the touch, smell, and being able to create something from harakeke. Weaving grounds me, connects me to my whakapapa, and grows my mind and spirit.” Giarne later started teaching raranga and sharing weaving tips at regular workshops, while being constantly mindful of the traditions behind the craft. “I understand why there is a lot of protectionism around weaving. It is important to honour that and take the right steps while continually building more intricate knowledge.” This year, Giarne has done fewer raranga workshops as her main focus has been her te reo Māori studies. She has also tried some new art projects, making clothing, as well as earrings, and even a kete from upcycled bicycle tyres. In her day job, as a member of the support staff in the Art Department at Hillmorton High School, Giarne occasionally does weaving with students from the school, which have been extremely rewarding, she says. “The girls all turn up. They want to be there, which makes me realise why I’m doing it. Art or some kind of creative expression can be a real release.” Giarne says her parents are supportive of her immersion into her Māori heritage, and she is excited by what is ahead in her ongoing journey of discovery. Meri Te Tai Mangakāhia
Meri Te Tai Mangakāhia was a campaigner for women's suffrage, and inspired future generations of Māori women.

Although she may not be as well-known as Kate Sheppard and the other suffragists from the late 1800s, another inspiring wāhine from this era is Meri Te Tai Mangakāhia (22 May 1868 – 10 October 1920) who was also a campaigner for women's suffrage, and inspired future generations of Māori women. Meri Te Tai is said to have been born on 22 May 1868, near Whakarapa (Panguru) on the Hokianga Harbour. Her great-grandmother was woman of mana, Ngākahuwhero, and her father, Rē Te Tai, was an influential chief of Te Rarawa in the Hokianga district. She is said to have been well-educated, studying at St Mary's Convent in Auckland, and she was an accomplished pianist. In the late 1880s or early 1890s she became the third wife of Hāmiora Mangakāhia, of Ngāti Whanaunga and other Coromandel hapū. He was an assessor in the Native Land Court, and was working at Waimate North in 1887. He was also at the Bay of Islands in 1889, attending the meeting at which Te Kotahitanga, the Māori parliament movement, was formally initiated. Hāmiora Mangakāhia was elected premier of the Kotahitanga parliament in June 1892. In 1893 both he and Meri attended the second session of the parliament at Waipatu in Hawke's Bay. The women's suffrage movement had been gaining strength from the 1880s, and it is likely that Meri had knowledge of this. She may, like many Māori women, have come into contact with the New Zealand Women's Christian Temperance Union, which campaigned for women's suffrage. On 18 May 1893 the Speaker of the lower house of the Kotahitanga parliament introduced a motion from Meri Mangakāhia, requesting that women be given the right to participate in the selection of members. It was suggested that she come into the house to explain her motion, and later that day she addressed the parliament – the first woman recorded to have done so. She requested not only that Māori women be given the vote, but that they be eligible to sit in the Māori parliament, thus going a step further than the contemporary aims of the European suffrage movement. She argued on the grounds that many Māori women owned and administered their own lands, either because they had no male relatives or because the women were more competent. She claimed that although chiefs had appealed to Queen Victoria over Māori problems, Māori women had received no advantage from these appeals, and suggested that the Queen might more readily respond to representations by women. Meri was followed in the debate by Ākenehi Tomoana, wife of Hēnare Tomoana, the host at Waipatu. Ākenehi suggested that discussion of the issue be postponed until the men had 'achieved their goal' – until, she appears to have meant, they had succeeded in achieving recognition for the Kotahitanga parliament. The matter then lapsed. Little further is recorded of Meri Mangakāhia's participation in the Kotahitanga movement, but she continued to be active in Māori politics and welfare. An oil portrait painted about this time, preserved by her family, shows a beautiful young woman dressed in the height of European fashion. It is likely that she was a member of one of the women's committees of the Kotahitanga movement. These committees, early forerunners of the Māori Women's Welfare League, organised the activities of young people attending Kotahitanga meetings, and undertook massive catering. They also held meetings and debated political issues. Meri and Hāmiora Mangakāhia spent most of their last years together at Whangapoua. Hāmiora died in June 1918, and Meri died of influenza on 10 October 1920, aged 52. She was buried at Pūreirei cemetery, Lower Waihou, near her father. Information sourced from teara.govt.nz

Easter recipe – Lemon Biscuits
Niinotchka McKay shares her personal love of Easter, and her favourite Easter recipe.

With Easter weekend upon us, we asked Ninotchka (Nin) McKay, a post graduate student from St Martins, to share why Easter is her favourite Christian holiday. Nin has also kindly provided a recipe for Lemon Biscuits, which she loves to make and decorate in an Easter theme, with her two children Isla (13) and Asher (5) who are pictured, having fun with lots of creative designs. “We all enjoy a good Easter egg hunt, and plenty of chocolate, but Easter is also a bittersweet time. It can be hard to translate the message behind this week for children without terrifying them. However I think it can be comforting for kids to pray to a Jesus who understands what it’s like to be misunderstood, teased or jeered on the playground, and it helps to set some context for all the ‘unfair’ stuff happening in the world right now.” Nin says the key message of Easter, that God’s overwhelming, undeniable love for his people is so big that he would sacrifice his son, then resurrect him with the promise of eternal life to all who follow him, is a powerful message that can help when the world feels broken. “I guess that’s why Easter features chickens and eggs – the idea of new life and a new start. Self-reflection and asking for forgiveness when you get it wrong is another hopeful and accessible message for anyone young or old.” Lemon Biscuits This biscuit is a great Easter recipe to make with children, and is perfect for everything from school lunches to elegant high tea. - 100g Butter (room temperature) - ½ teaspoon salt - Zest of a small lemon - 1 cup sugar - 1 egg - 2 tablespoons milk - 2 cups of flour - 1 teaspoon baking powder - ½ teaspoon baking soda Preheat oven to 180oC (not fan forced). Line two cookie sheets with non-stick baking paper. Cream first four ingredients until pale and fluffy. (A stand mixer is great if you have it) Whisk together milk and egg. Sift dry ingredients. Add milk/egg mixture in three batches, along with a teaspoon of the sifted dry ingredients each time (this is so the mixture doesn’t split). Mix well after each addition. Add the remaining dry ingredients and mix until a soft dough forms. Be aware that mixing too much at this stage will give you a ‘tough’ biscuit. I prefer to do this stage by hand. Dump out the dough onto a floured surface – it doesn’t matter if you have some dried ingredients not quite mixed at the bottom of the bowl. Form it together into a flat-ish ball. Using clingfilm or baking paper, wrap the dough and chill it in the fridge for about 10-15 mins. When your dough is chilled, roll it out to around 1cm thick and cut out biscuit shapes. If you don’t have a cutter, you could roll it into a sausage shape and cut slices to form your biscuits. Bake for 8-10mins until lightly golden and cool completely before adding your decorations. We used a ‘writing icing’ pack with several small coloured pre-mixed icings from the supermarket, hundreds and thousands and mini marshmallows to create their easter themed biscuits. These are lovely just plain in lunchboxes or they are a brilliant ‘dunker’ with a hot drink.

A voice from the past – suffragist Kate Sheppard
Renowned suffragist Kate Sheppard's legacy continues to impact women over 120 years after New Zealand women got the vote. Just who was Kate Sheppard?

It is easy to be moved when standing in the tasteful dining room at Te Whare Waiutuutu Kate Sheppard House, imagining the spirit of Kate Sheppard, the other suffragists, and leading figures for women’s rights who gathered there to plot, plan and create what proved to be the epicentre of the women’s global suffrage movement. We can also only imagine the joy, pride, and likely some relief, Kate must have felt when she was in her garden on 19 September 1893 and received the telegram advising her that women had finally had won the right to vote in New Zealand. The arduous five-year nationwide campaign she had led, had finally achieved its goal, against fierce opposition. So who was Kate Sheppard? The well-known images of Kate evoke a genteel, beautifully dressed, middle class Victorian woman, which was true of her position in Christchurch society in the late 1800s. However, it was her challenging early years in the United Kingdom which really forged her character and strong sense of social justice. Kate was born in Liverpool in 1847, to Andrew and Jemima Malcolm, who both had Scottish heritage. The family moved around a lot, eventually to London, where when Kate was just four years old, her father disappeared from their lives forever. The family always said he died 10 years later in Dublin, when in fact he had sailed to New York to join the US Army. He died of delirium tremens in New Mexico in 1862, but the family never breathed a word of the secret. Andrew had left the family one step away from homelessness, so Kate was sent to live with her maternal aunt and uncle, William Barclay, who was a Minister with the independent Free Church. With them, Kate gained a good education and developed a sense of standing up for things that weren’t right. Kate migrated to New Zealand aged 20. Her sister Marie had married drapery merchant, George Beath, who set up Beath’s Department Store in Christchurch. Kate married George’s friend, Walter Sheppard in 1871. George Beath paid for his wife’s family to sail to New Zealand first class. Kate and Walter’s only child, a son Douglas was born in 1880. In 1885, the Women’s Christian Temperance Movement hit New Zealand, when Mary Leavitt arrived from the United States to lecture New Zealand women about prohibition of alcohol. Kate signed up and was soon the National Superintendent of Franchise and Legislation, which included questioning laws around women. This was the beginning of her work as a suffragist. During the important campaign years from 1888 to 1893, Kate was living at the Clyde Road house, busily writing articles and letters, organising and sending out pamphlets, preparing speeches, lobbying members of parliament and directing other suffragists and women’s organisations throughout New Zealand. It was around Kate’s dining table that the third, and ultimately successful, large petition, consisting of 31,872 signatures gathered from women all around New Zealand, was pasted onto a large roll by Kate and other suffragists, and sent to the House of Representatives in Wellington. Their victory was a world-first achievement and led to many other women around the world gaining the right to vote. The New Zealand suffragists also wanted women to be able to get into Parliament so they could start making social reforms for women and children. Kate was known as a very good speaker, who would gently win her arguments through humour and using her intelligence. She was able to rally people and get them on board. She was also a fashionista, whose weakness was beautiful clothes. In 1902, nine years after the vote for women was won, Kate and Walter Sheppard decided to sell their Clyde Road house and return to England. Walter travelled with their son, Douglas, while Kate took an apartment in the centre of Christchurch before leaving in 1903. Kate’s health deteriorated, and on medical advice she returned to Christchurch, amicably parting with Walter, and living with her great friends, William and Jennie Lovell-Smith. Kate had largely retired from public speaking and activism by this time apart from writing and later supporting suffragists in the United Kingdom. Tragedy struck in 1910, when her son Douglas died of pernicious anaemia at the age of just 29, followed by her only grandchild, Margaret at the age of 21 in 1930. Kate married William Lovell-Smith in 1925, a year after he was widowed. In 1933, a year before Kate passed away, Elizabeth McCombs of Lyttelton, became the first women elected to Parliament. It had been Kate’s hope that women would eventually be represented in Parliament, so it was wonderful she saw it happen in her lifetime and her legacy came full circle.

Nurse Maude
The story of the real ‘Nurse Maude’ and her incredible legacy is truly fascinating. She was a genuine trailblazer.

Most of us would have seen the Nurse Maude cars zipping around Christchurch, driven by caring district nurses and palliative care nurses doing amazing work in our community. Did you now that the wonderful work of the ‘original’ Nurse Maude – a Christchurch nurse named Sibylla Emily Maude, led to the birth of district nursing in Christchurch and around New Zealand? Born in 1862, the eldest of eight children, Sibylla grew up in a deeply religious Anglican family. The example set by her father, who was at the time of her birth, Provincial Secretary, and two of her aunts who were nurses appears to have inspired in her a desire to help other people. She rose to the role of matron of Christchurch Hospital by 1893 but left that role in 1896 to begin the work for which she was most remembered – caring for the old, infirm and poor in their own homes. Nurse Maude got financial backing to undertake district nursing in the city, and established a headquarters in Durham Street, which served as a dispensary and a place where minor ailments were treated. In 1901, the District Nursing Association was founded to support the work. Dressed in a light-blue uniform and white apron with dark-blue cape and bonnet, Nurse Maude walked many miles every day in all weathers, carrying not only nursing equipment but often the pans for cooking, cleaning and washing which many people lacked. She tried to teach her patients the importance of cleanliness and fresh air, and as a woman of prayer she invariably prayed with them. More nurses joined – and a horse and cart and bicycles were provided to transport them. Nurse Maude opened two sanitoriums for tuberculosis in Christchurch and during the influenza epidemic which started in 1918, was put in charge of the central bureau to coordinate the nursing of influenza victims in the city. In the difficult 1920s she opened a soup kitchen for undernourished children. Nurse Maude never married and died on 12 July 1935. Her funeral was attended by large crowds who came to pay tribute to the most celebrated women in the city at the time. She was greatly respected and loved for her genuine practical sympathy for the poor and her complete integrity. Thanks to the impact of her work, district nursing was developed throughout the country.
